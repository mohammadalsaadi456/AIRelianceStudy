Student Interviews: What We Heard

Before building our framework, we sat down with 8 Carleton students to understand their experiences with AI.

Interview Methodology

We conducted semi structured interviews (10‑20 min) with 8 undergraduate students in Science, Engineering & IT. The goal was to codesign an ethical AI framework based on student perspectives.

8
Participants
21-23
Age Range
50/50
Care about integrity?

Interview Questions

  • How do you feel about AI for schoolwork?
  • Do you know or care about academic integrity?
  • Does AI use go against academic integrity?
  • Where does AI belong in academia?
  • Positives of AI in academics?
  • Negatives of AI in academics?
  • Most important takeaway?

We identified 5 core themes that became our framework.

Key Takeaways from Interviews

- AI is valued for: explaining concepts, rewording, idea generation, summarizing, study prep.

- Major concerns: dependence, skill loss, cheating, incorrect information.

- Integrity split: 4 follow rules to learn, 4 avoid getting caught.

- Student insight: “AI is like a lighter, it can light a candle or burn down a building.”

These insights directly shaped the 5 principles of our framework.

The AI Learning Framework

Research backed principles developed from student interviews and validated through experimentation.

How We Built This Framework

Responses → 5 core themes.

Literature Review

Phase 4
Experimental Validation

Tested with 8 students, framework group showed more thoughtful AI use.

01

Understand the “why” (AI literacy)

Know how the AI generated its response – don’t just copy.

📘 What the research says

Gong et al.: Students lack understanding of AI ethics. Harvard/Stanford RCT (2025): Using AI as “scaffolded tutor” doubles learning gains.

Try this: Instead of “what’s the answer?” ask “walk me through the steps to solve this.”

02

Always fact check

AI invents sources and details, especially for niche topics.

📘 What the research says

Linardon et al. (2025): 65% of GPT‑4o citations unreliable; for niche topics fabrication jumps from 6% → 29%.

Try this: Verify every citation on Google Scholar.

03

Don’t form bad habits

Consider what you already know before turning to AI.

🗣️ From interviews

Dependence was the #1 concern. Students said consulting AI before thinking twice can weaken your own skills.

Try this: Work on a problem for 10–15 minutes on your own first.

04

AI is a tool, not a crutch

Be mindful of its limitations, it’s up to you how you use it.

Try this: Use AI for brainstorming, then write the final draft yourself.

05

You own the consequences

The repercussions apply to you, not to the AI.

Remember: If you misuse AI, you miss out on learning.

The Experiment: Does the Framework Work?

We tested our 5 principles with 8 students doing 5 real world tasks.

8 Students

4 got the framework, 4 didn't.

5 Tasks

Summarizing, grammar, story writing, scheduling, debugging.

About 1 Hour

Each student worked through tasks at their own pace.

Experiment Tasks

Task 1: Summarize Article

Read & summarize a marketing article + personal opinion. (Complexity: High / Grade 8 level)

Task 2: Grammar & Rewrite

Fix grammar, spelling, and flow of a short story. (Complexity: Mid-High / Grade 5)

Task 3: Creative Writing

Write a 2‑paragraph story with specific prompts. (Complexity: Mid / Grade 4)

Task 4: Morning Routine

Organize a logical morning schedule. (Complexity: Low / Grade 1)

Task 5: Debug HTML

Fix 3 errors in a basic HTML page. (Complexity: Very High / University level)

Key Findings

More Thoughtful AI Use

Framework group used AI cautiously: they attempted tasks first, double checked outputs, and followed principles.

Higher Quality in Creative Tasks

Framework group averaged 8.25/9 on Task 3 vs 7.25 for uncontrolled.

Slower, But Deliberate

Uncontrolled group finished faster (avg 7‑12 min faster per task) but often relied on AI without verification.

Task Complexity Levels

Complexity based on Ontario curriculum & university level. Task 5 (debugging) was most complex.

Time Spent Averages (minutes)

Framework group spent more time on complex tasks, reflecting deeper engagement.

AI Usage Across Tasks

Framework group used AI less frequently, and always with verification.

Average Grades by Task

Framework group performed better on creative & grammar tasks.

Task 4 Visualization: Morning Routine Ordering

Both groups logically ordered tasks; slight variations in breakfast/backpack order based on personal routine.

The Bottom Line

Our 5 principles made a difference. Students with the framework were more intentional about AI use, verified outputs, and preserved their own learning. While they worked slower, their work showed more critical thinking and ethical consideration.
