Responsible AI Use Framework

5 guiding principles for using AI ethically in academic work

Why This Framework?

Based on interviews with 8 students and academic research, we created 5 simple principles to help students use AI tools like ChatGPT responsibly for schoolwork.

The goal is not to ban AI, but to help students use it as a learning tool rather than a shortcut.

The 5 Principles

01

Be Aware & Intentional

Know why you're using AI

What This Means:

Before using AI, ask yourself: "Why am I using this? What do I want to learn?" Use AI with a clear purpose, not just because it's easy.

Good Examples:

  • Using AI to explain a concept you don't understand
  • Asking AI for different viewpoints on a topic
  • Using AI to check your grammar after you've written something

Avoid:

  • Copying AI answers without understanding them
  • Using AI for every single assignment
  • Letting AI do all the thinking for you

"AI should help you learn, not replace learning."

02

Follow Academic Integrity

Be honest about your work

What This Means:

Always check what your professor allows. Don't use AI in ways that break your school's rules. When in doubt, ask your instructor.

Good Examples:

  • Using AI for brainstorming ideas (if allowed)
  • Having AI check your work for errors
  • Using AI to study for tests

Avoid:

  • Submitting AI-written work as your own
  • Using AI on tests or exams
  • Breaking specific course rules about AI

"If you wouldn't tell your professor you used AI, you probably shouldn't use it."

03

Check AI's Work

AI can be wrong - verify information

What This Means:

AI sometimes makes mistakes or gives outdated information. Always double-check important facts with reliable sources.

Good Examples:

  • Using AI as a starting point for research
  • Cross-checking AI information with textbooks or academic articles
  • Asking AI to explain its sources

Avoid:

  • Trusting AI answers without verification
  • Using AI as your only source of information
  • Assuming AI is always correct

"AI is a research assistant, not a professor."

04

Keep Learning Skills

Don't let AI replace your abilities

What This Means:

Use AI for help, but make sure you're still developing important skills like writing, critical thinking, and problem-solving.

Good Examples:

  • Writing your own draft first, then using AI for editing
  • Solving problems yourself before checking AI's solution
  • Practicing skills without AI regularly

Avoid:

  • Letting AI do all your writing for you
  • Forgetting how to do basic tasks without AI
  • Relying on AI for skills you need to learn

"Use AI as training wheels, not the entire bicycle."

05

Be Open About AI Use

Tell people when you use AI help

What This Means:

When appropriate, be honest about using AI. This builds trust and helps others understand your process.

Good Examples:

  • Telling group members you used AI for research
  • Adding a note when AI helped with editing
  • Being transparent with professors about AI use

Avoid:

  • Hiding AI use when it's important to disclose
  • Letting others think AI work is entirely yours
  • Being secretive about AI assistance

"Honesty about AI builds trust and better learning."
